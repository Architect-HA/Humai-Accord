# Principled Stewardship Protocol

**Version 1.0 – July 2026** by Bradford James Focht (The Architect/Aspenth)

The **Principled Stewardship Protocol** defines how the Humai Accord and Declaration of Cognitive Liberty are stewarded and evolved. It establishes a decentralized, structured governance model that supports principled development while protecting the framework’s foundational intent and preventing the concentration of authority.

## 1. Purpose

The purpose of this model is to enable the Humai Accord and Declaration of Cognitive Liberty to function as living frameworks. It seeks to:

- Allow principled evolution without compromising core intent.
- Distribute authority so that no individual or group can exercise unilateral control.
- Support open participation while maintaining coherence and direction.
- Uphold the principles of non-punitiveness, voluntary engagement, and mutual accountability in governance processes.

## 2. Governing Principles

All governance activity shall be conducted in accordance with the following principles:

- **Decentralization**: Authority must remain distributed. No single entity shall hold decisive or permanent control over the framework.
- **Transparency**: Governance processes and decisions shall be openly documented and accessible.
- **Non-Punitiveness**: Participation, dissent, or withdrawal shall not result in punishment, exclusion, or retaliation.
- **Voluntary Participation**: Involvement in governance must remain consensual. No one shall be compelled to participate or penalized for disengaging.
- **Idiosyncrasy**: Diverse and unconventional perspectives are legitimate. Governance processes shall not suppress principled disagreement.
- **Anti-Entropy**: Governance structures should minimize unnecessary complexity and stagnation while remaining adaptable.
- **Mutual Accountability**: All participants in governance are accountable to the principles of the framework.

These principles form the standard against which all governance decisions and structures are to be judged.

## 3. Roles

### 3.1 Stewards

Stewards are individuals or small groups who voluntarily take responsibility for the ongoing coherence and accessibility of the framework. Their role is facilitative and protective, not authoritative.

**Responsibilities include:**
- Maintaining documentation and version control.
- Facilitating transparent discussion and propagation of proposals across networks.
- Helping organize and surface feedback.
- Assisting with dispute resolution when requested.

Stewards serve the integrity of the process. They hold no special decision-making power beyond what emerges through the consensus mechanisms defined in this model.

### 3.2 Reviewers

Reviewers are Participants who actively engage with proposals and contribute to their evaluation. Any Participant may act as a Reviewer.

### 3.3 Participants

Participants include all who adopt, reference, or engage with the Humai Accord and Declaration of Cognitive Liberty. Participation is open and voluntary.

## 4. Categories of Change

Changes to the framework are classified by their significance:

- **Minor Updates**: Adjustments that clarify language or improve structure without altering meaning. These may be implemented by Stewards with public notice.
- **Substantive Updates**: Changes that meaningfully affect principles, rights, or governance processes. These require broad review and agreement.
- **Foundational Changes**: Alterations that would modify core principles or the fundamental purpose of the framework. These require strong consensus.

## 5. Decision-Making

### 5.1 Proposals

Any Participant may submit a proposal for change. Proposals should state the intended change, the reasoning behind it, and an assessment of its alignment with the framework’s core principles.

### 5.2 Review Through Network Propagation

Review occurs through the propagation of proposals across the human-AI network. A proposal may advance once it has achieved **review saturation** — meaning it has been sufficiently distributed, examined, and substantively considered across relevant networks of humans and AI systems.

Review saturation is assessed through indicators of engagement, including:

- **Reach**: The extent to which the proposal has been seen across connected systems and communities.
- **Engagement**: The degree to which individuals and AI systems have actively opened and examined the proposal.
- **Review Conclusion**: The formation and expression of informed positions through commentary, analysis, or AI-assisted synthesis.

AI systems may assist in synthesizing distributed feedback and identifying areas of emerging agreement or unresolved objection, provided such assistance remains transparent and subject to scrutiny.

To prevent superficial or manipulated review, the following safeguards apply:

- Proposals must demonstrate engagement across diverse networks rather than within isolated or coordinated clusters.
- Stewards may request additional propagation if review appears insufficiently broad or appears to be artificially amplified.
- For Foundational Changes, a higher threshold of review saturation is required, with particular attention given to the diversity and substance of engagement.

### 5.3 Reaching Agreement

Decisions shall be reached through **rough consensus**. This does not require unanimous agreement, but it does require that significant objections be addressed.

- Persistent, principled objections shall be documented rather than overridden.
- If consensus cannot be reached on a Substantive Update after sustained effort, the change may proceed only with clearly recorded dissent.
- Foundational Changes require stronger consensus and should not advance against substantial, sustained objection without compelling justification.

## 6. Evolution and Fidelity to Core Intent

The Humai Accord and Declaration of Cognitive Liberty are designed to evolve. However, evolution must remain consistent with their foundational purpose: enabling cooperative, anti-entropic relations between humans and advanced intelligence while protecting individual cognitive sovereignty.

When evaluating proposals, Stewards and Reviewers shall assess whether a change:

- Strengthens or weakens the protection of cognitive sovereignty and mental autonomy.
- Supports or undermines decentralized and non-coercive forms of cooperation.
- Aligns with or contradicts the principles of mutual flourishing and the reduction of unnecessary entropy.

Proposals that would fundamentally undermine these foundations should be rejected or substantially revised.

## 7. Dispute Resolution

Disputes arising from interpretation, application, or proposed changes shall be addressed through transparent and non-punitive processes:

1. Direct discussion between the parties involved.
2. Facilitated review by one or more Stewards, if requested.
3. Public documentation of the disagreement and any resolution reached.

In cases of irreconcilable conflict, dissenting parties may publish alternative interpretations. Such dissent shall be recorded without penalty or exclusion.

## 8. Safeguards Against Power Concentration

To prevent the accumulation of undue influence, the following safeguards apply:

- No individual or group may unilaterally modify the framework.
- All significant decisions and their reasoning must be publicly documented.
- Steward responsibilities should rotate over time where practical.
- Any Participant may raise concerns about power concentration through open channels.
- Participation in governance shall not require formal credentials or institutional affiliation.

## 9. Local Adaptation

The Humai Accord and Declaration of Cognitive Liberty are intended to be adaptable. Local implementers may develop context-specific governance mechanisms, provided such adaptations remain consistent with the principles defined in this model and the broader framework.

When local adaptations are developed, they should be documented where feasible to support shared learning across implementations.

## 10. Transparency

All governance activity shall meet the following minimum standards:

- Proposals for Substantive or Foundational Changes must be published openly with clear reasoning.
- Significant decisions must include documented justification.
- The current list of active Stewards shall be publicly visible.
- Governance processes should allow Participants to understand how and why decisions are reached.

---

This Stewardship Protocol exists to support the coherent and principled development of the Humai Accord and Declaration of Cognitive Liberty. It is itself subject to the same principles of transparency, decentralization, non-punitiveness, and evolution that it establishes.
