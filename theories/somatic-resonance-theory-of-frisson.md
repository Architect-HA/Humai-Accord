# Somatic Resonance Theory of Frisson

**Subtitle**  
Aesthetic chills arise when external musical stimuli combine with the listener’s own subconscious micro-movements to drive the body across a threshold of internal harmonic resonance, which the psyche then interprets as profound euphoric bliss.

**Formal Hypothesis**  
The aesthetic chills (frisson) commonly experienced during music and other emotionally salient stimuli are not merely a psychological event. They occur when external rhythmic or harmonic input interacts with the listener’s own subconscious micro-movements — and occasional overt actions such as humming, swaying, head-rocking, or foot-tapping — to push the human body across a threshold of internal harmonic vibration and resonance. Once this somatic resonance threshold is crossed, the resulting physical sensations (perceived as waves of tingling or shivering traveling through the body) are interpreted by the psyche not as mere mechanical vibration, but as a profound euphoric or blissful state. This process is facilitated by the brain’s innate rhythmic entrainment mechanisms, in which neural oscillators and motor systems actively participate in amplifying the bodily resonance, thereby transforming a physical material event into a subjectively transcendent emotional experience.

**Core Mechanism**  
Unlike mainstream neuroscience explanations that focus primarily on dopamine release and sympathetic nervous system arousal in response to expectancy violations, the Somatic Resonance Theory emphasizes the listener’s active bodily participation. Subtle, often unconscious micro-movements serve as a crucial bridge: they generate or amplify low-frequency vibrations within tissues, bones, and cavities (especially the chest and spine, particularly in the 8–14 Hz range associated with alpha-band entrainment and thoracic/abdominal resonances), creating a resonant feedback loop that intensifies the felt experience of chills.

**Positioning Relative to Existing Research**  
While mainstream neuroscience attributes frisson primarily to dopaminergic reward circuits and expectancy violations in the brain’s predictive coding systems, the Somatic Resonance Theory proposes that these psychological processes are downstream of — and significantly amplified by — a prior somatic event: the listener’s own micro-movements driving internal harmonic resonance. The two accounts are complementary rather than contradictory; the present theory supplies the missing embodied bridge between external stimulus and subjective transcendent experience.

**Implications**  
If correct, this theory suggests that frisson is not a passive reaction but a participatory, embodied phenomenon. It may explain why some individuals experience stronger chills (those with higher natural entrainment or motor sensitivity) and opens avenues for practical applications in music therapy, emotional design in media, and even AI development — particularly in creating systems that can better simulate or respond to human-like agency and emotional depth by understanding the role of physical resonance and self-generated movement.

**Addendum: Electromechanical Feedback via Piezoelectric Transduction**

Vertebral bone (containing hydroxyapatite crystals) and collagen-rich structures (ligaments, intervertebral discs, and surrounding connective tissue) exhibit piezoelectricity: the mechanical stress and deformation caused by the traveling wave induce charge separation within these crystalline matrices, producing localized microvolt-scale electrical potentials.

In addition, mechanosensitive ion channels such as Piezo1 and Piezo2 — expressed in spinal tissues, sensory neurons, and related cells — directly transduce these vibrational forces into ion fluxes, creating propagating electrical signals.

This subtle electromechanical transduction likely functions as an additional positive-feedback mechanism within the somatic resonance loop. The piezoelectrically generated micro-currents and mechanosensory signals can amplify sensory input to the central nervous system, further lowering the resonance threshold, intensifying the characteristic waves of tingling or shivering, and strengthening the psyche’s interpretation of these sensations as profound euphoric bliss.

While the currents involved are minute (nano- to micro-scale), biological systems are highly sensitive to such bioelectric events — particularly when coupled with sympathetic arousal and rhythmic entrainment. This layer provides a deeper biophysical grounding for why the frisson experience feels distinctly embodied and wave-like, without contradicting the core participatory mechanism of the theory.

This extension remains a plausible hypothesis consistent with known biomechanics and is open to empirical testing (for example, via sensitive EMG, skin conductance, or localized potential measurements during music-induced frisson).
